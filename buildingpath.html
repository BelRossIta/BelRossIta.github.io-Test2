What You Should Know About Building Computers.

It really is easy to do.

When colleges students and parents flock to reddit and other forums looking for information on what type of computer to purchase for themselves or for their child, most desktop users will tell them to build their own rather than buying a prebuilt as they will be able to save a good amount of money and purchase better parts, though it is always recommend that if you are not confident or just do not want to take the chance of breaking or destroying something buying a prebuilt is the way to go.

Having said that, a common saying that is told to new pc builder before they start building is that putting together a computer is similar to putting together legos: You guys put the right part in the right place where the manual tell you to place it then plug the power supply into the computer then turn it on. If everything goes right, static shock doesn't kill your part, and you buy the correct part, you will have a working computer in no time.

It really is hard to do

Well, it sounds easy doesn't it? Just place everything together like legos and everything will work. Well sometimes you can put everything in the right place with the right parts that match the board and work with the cpu and you can still have problems getting the computer to turn on OR you can get the computer to turn on but you won't get anything to output to the monitor not matter what you do. It could be that you have the monitor plugged into the motherboard when you should have it plugged in to the graphics card, that is if you are lucky. A worse situation would be the computer turning on but not outputting to the monitor and you have the monitor plugged in to the right port.

Things can go wrong and not work for no reason at all or it could be a niche reason that you, a beginner pc builder, would not think would be a problem.

I just want a black box- The Everlasting Quest for the Perfect Part

Sometimes finding the right part for what you need is really easy but lately with the silicon shortage, energy crisis, steel price increase, and other things, finding the right part for the right price is harder than what is used to be especially after a launch of a new generation of CPUs. Then there are the scalpers that come when there are items that are in stock and come to buy large amounts of parts to resell at higher prices. This happens a lot and also happens in the gaming field with the latest example being the PS5.

But lets say that you don't want the newest toy on the block. There should be older parts that you can buy, right? Maybe a older gen platform that still runs well and isn't really that old for what you are looking for. You should have no problem get the items you need, right? Well, not so. Even if the platform was one generation ago and the company ran that gen only three years ago, distributors could still be having problem with keeping stock, weather it is too many people purchasing the item or the company that makes them is not making enough of them, there can still be stock problems.

With all of that said, there is still the problem of personal choice. For example, most the most common type of case that is available on the market will be one that has a tempered glass window on it and it is rather hard to find a current generation case that is just a simple case with no window. Now you might ask, why would someone not what a tempered glass window on there case? Well the answer to that is that many of these window tend to crack, if not shatter, at random times and there isn't really a brand or manufacturer that is worse or better than another, all of them do it at one point or another.

Plus many people just prefer the simple, clean look. Due to this, many have to look for older cases that have the solid side panels that they want but the cases that are solid and also have good features like good air circulation or good hard drive support may not be in stock in a lot of online or ever brick-in-mortar stores. That or they have increase in price due to the manufacturer lower production of that particular case and thus lowering online distributors stock of it. Another thing that could cause it to be hard to find the right case would be popularity. With the advent of so many tempered glass case failure, solid panel cases have gained a bit of popularity and that could also cause a increase of price and lowering of stock. The Building Blocks of Building a Computer

Before getting into details, lets list down a overview:

  1. Set Your Budget
  2. Research performance of and choose platform(AMD or Intel) based on needs/wants
  3. Research and purchase the Cpu you need for specifications or want Example: A gaming computer will benefit from a faster Cpu.
  4. Research and purchase the gpu that fits your needs
  5. Research and purchase the memory that you need for your cpu
  6. Research and purchase the power supply that you may need(I always get 150-200 watts more for peripherals )
  7. Purchase the fans that fit your budget
  8. Spend the rest of budget on monitor, keyboard, mouse and hard drive/solid state drive

This is the first step that you should taking when beginning the path of building your computer. Though you could switch this with step 2 as number 1 but I think it is better to know how much you are willing to spend based on the money you have available or have saved up rather then picking the platform first.

In today's economy, based on what you need/want, you can get away with a 450 dollars and this is for a entry level gaming computer build on the AMD platform. I chose the AMD platform because, honestly, AMD has really picked up the pace with their Ryzen line and they have been known for the lower prices, at least compared to Intel, for a while. They still give a good price-to-performance ratio or you can go as high as you want but I wouldn't go beyond 2500 dollars but I've seen some insanely high priced computers. It's all what you want to do.

Research and Purchase The Power Supply That You May Need

When it comes to figuring out which power supply to get, you should first figure out how much power you need for you computer. Many power supply sellers have calculators on their websites that you can use to give you a rough estimate and pcpartpicker is a website that you can use to build a computer list and it will list an approximate wattage that all of the parts will be. You also need to take in to consideration, when choosing you psu, gpu power consumption. For example: 12+4 pin connectors on the upcoming and some current generation graphics cards will use will provide up to 600w of power. At the moment, a pair of 6+2 pin connector on dedicated cables are rate for 300w and three of these connector can deliver up to 450w safely.

The main issue that power supplies are said to have isn't the maximum sustained power consumption of a GPU but the power spikes that can happen. That is why many manufacturers suggest strong PSU for high-tiered graphics cards.

Another thing to consider will be the physical dimensions of your case before you purchase it. This goes hand-in-hand with all of the parts of your computer but especially your GPU. The reason being is that there are different sizes of power supplies like ATX and SFX power supplies. If you get a standard ATX case, whether or not is it high-tier or not, at ATX power supply will fit but keep in mind many higher-wattage PSU are longer th an the typical 5.5 inches so be sure to look at the the measurements of the PSU area. If you have a really small or slim PC case, you will want to get a more compact SFX power supply.

Last thing to consider: A modular power supply. If you decided to get a case that has a lot of room behind the motherboard, or your case doesn't have window or glass side, you can tie the wire you don't need and stash them inside your rig but if the system you're building doesn't have space then it's worth investing in a modular power supply. Modular PSUs allow you to only plug in the power cables you need and leave the rest in the box.

Pc Cases/Chassis

Cases, or Chassis, can come in different shapes and sizes with some even holding more than one motherboard, allowing for a two computer build to be in one case. When you start to look around for one, you need to look out for the following terms as they are used to identify not only the type of case but also the type of motherboard the case is designed to accommodate as well as implying that for the cases size might hold a bigger motherboard and vice versa:

  • Mini-ITX: Supports Motherboards measuring 6.75 x 6.75 inches
  • MicroATX: Supports motherboards measuring a maximum of 9.63 x 9.63 inches
  • ATX: Supports Motherboards measuring 12 x 9.63 inches(LxW)
  • XL/EATX: Supports Motherboards measuring 12 x 13 inches(LxW)

Make sure to take note that most cases can support smaller motherboards type. An example would be the fact that many ATX cases can support Mini-ITX or MicroATX motherboards.There are also some other terms that you might see used to describe cases when you shopping for one:

  • Desktop - Horizontal Design. Sometimes monitors sit on top of a desktop case/chassis
  • SFF - Stands for Small Form Factor. These are compact chassis for accommodating mini-ATX or smaller proprietary motherboards
  • Mini-Tower - Standard Vertical Tower. No specific size but smaller than mid-tower and full tower
  • Mid-Tower - Standard Vertical Tower. No specific size but bigger than mini-tower and smaller than full tower
  • Full Tower - Usually 18 inches tall or more

Different cases have different features from one another. Some have only 2.5 inch drive bay for SSDs. Others have only 3.5 inch bays for hard drives and there are some other cases that have, in addition to 2.5 or 3.5 drive bays, a 5.25 drive bay for optical drives. Then there are cases that have a mixture of both or all three. There are also cases that have different input/output shields for different port and various case fan or water-cooling support capabilities.

For my last two build, I went with Fractal Design for my cases due to drive bay support, silence, and the fact that I could get cases with solid side panels rather than one with tempered glass.

Case Fans

PC or case fans are the first defense against heat protection when it comes to thermal regulation in your computer. Some of the best PC fans can keep even the most intense gaming rig cool. Most of the time, however they are rather overlooked by builders. Cool hardware is the most important thing when it comes to the longevity of your computer and fans are so important for that, that many times computer repairers will first check to see if the fans are working properly if there are temperature problems with a computer. Plus, it's not just keeping the parts cools, it's also maintaining and ensuring good airflow as well. Noctua are some of the best case/pc fans on the market and there are also some Corsair fans that are also very good. With your mouse, you need a keyboard and for keyboards there are two types: Membrane and Mechanical.

Membrane

Membrane Keyboards are ones that use a rubber or silicone membrane beneath the keys to register keystrokes. These types of keyboards are the most common type of keyboards on the market today. Within each key of a membrane keyboard, there is a small rubber or silicone beneath it. When a key is pressed, the dome falls which presses a circuit underneath it and registers the keystrokes. There are many advantages to these types of keyboards with them being less expensive than other types of keyboards being one of them. They are also lightweight, thin, and quiet, making them a good choice to use in public or shared spaces. A negative, however, is that they are not as durable or as reliable as other types of keyboards.

Mechanical

Mechanical keyboards are a keyboard type that use mechanical switches beneath each key to register each keystroke. These switches are more durable ane more reliable than the rubber or silicone domes used in membrane keyboards. In addition, they provide a more tactile and audible feedback to the users. Mechanical switches use different mechanisms to register keystrokes, but they all share a few key characteristics. Each switch has a metal spring and a stem that press against a metal contact, completing an electrical circuit and registering the keystroke. The spring gives a tactile feedback and the coveted click that many lovers of mechanical keyboards love.

One advantage that this type of keyboard has over membrane is going to be durability. The switches that are used in the keyboards are rated for tens of millions of keystrokes, making them a good choice for those who type a lot or who want a keyboard that will last a long time. Another positive is that they are customizable, with the ability on some of the keyboards to hot-swap the switches out with different ones. One negative, however, is that they tend to be on the expensive side of things with the cheaper ones being around 159. Add on to that the fact that they need much more maintenance than membrane, with the switches becoming dirty or worn with time and needing to be cleaned or replaced.
